# Protocol 121: Fill Cells — CMU Grout Placement Verification

> **Inspection Type:** Structural — Masonry Construction
> **Version:** 1.0
> **Jurisdiction Basis:** Florida Building Code (FBC), 8th Edition; TMS 402/602 (Building Code Requirements and Specification for Masonry Structures)
> **Author/Reviewer PE:** [Oasis Engineering]
> **Revision Date:** 2026-03-24

---

## 1) Scope

Construction-phase verification of grout placement in designated CMU (concrete masonry unit) cells for residential and light commercial masonry construction. This protocol covers the inspection window **during the grouting operation** — after reinforcement is placed and before grout sets — as well as the pre-grout readiness check.

This is a **construction-phase** inspection. Grout placement is a one-time, irreversible operation. The time to verify grout adequacy is during the pour — not after curing.

### In Scope
- Pre-grout cell readiness (cleanout, reinforcement position, cell obstructions).
- Grout material verification (type, slump, delivery documentation).
- Grout placement observation (lift height, consolidation, timing).
- Verification that all cells designated on the structural plans receive grout.
- Distinction between structural grout cells and mortar joints between masonry units.

### Out of Scope
- Footing inspection (see [Protocol 101](./101_footer_inspection.md)).
- Footer bond / Ufer ground inspection (see [Protocol 200](../ELECTRICAL/200_footer_bond_inspection.md)).
- Post-construction assessment of grout adequacy in existing masonry (requires separate scope, methods, and limitations).
- Mortar mix design or mortar joint quality (separate masonry inspection item).
- Structural design review of the masonry system.

---

## 2) Objective

Verify that grout placement in designated CMU cells meets the structural plans and applicable code by confirming:
- **PASS:** All designated cells are grouted to the required height with proper consolidation, reinforcement is correctly positioned, and grout material meets specifications.
- **HOLD:** Minor corrections needed before grout placement proceeds (e.g., cell debris, mispositioned bar, delivery ticket discrepancy).
- **FAIL / STOP WORK:** Designated cells not grouted, grout placed without consolidation, reinforcement missing from required cells, or grout material does not meet specifications.

## 5) Safety + Non-Destructive Limits

- **Do not** lean on, push, or apply lateral force to ungrouted masonry walls. Ungrouted CMU walls are structurally unstable and can collapse.
- **Do not** reach into cells or place hands inside the wall cavity.
- **Do not** stand on or walk on ungrouted block walls.
- Use flashlight and mirror to inspect cell interiors from above — do not insert tools that could dislodge reinforcement or block.
- **Stop condition:** If any designated cell cannot be verified as clear and ready for grout (obstructed, debris-filled, reinforcement missing), that cell must be corrected before grout placement proceeds.

## 6) Preconditions

- [ ] Masonry units are laid to the height of the planned grout lift.
- [ ] Structural plans are on-site identifying which cells are designated for grout and reinforcement.
- [ ] Vertical reinforcement is placed in all designated cells per the plans.
- [ ] Cleanout openings (if required by wall height) are in place and accessible.
- [ ] Grout delivery is scheduled and batch/delivery tickets will be available.
- [ ] Mortar joints are set sufficiently to resist grout pressure (typically minimum 4 hours for standard mortar, per TMS 602).
- [ ] Weather conditions are within acceptable range for grouting (not freezing, not extreme heat without mitigation).

## 7) Procedure Steps

### Step 1: Review the Structural Plans — Identify Designated Cells

1. Before any grout is placed, review the sealed structural plans and identify:
   - **Which cells are designated for grout** (typically cells with vertical reinforcement, bond beam courses, and any cells called out as "grouted solid" on the plans).
   - **Which cells are NOT designated for grout** (some masonry designs use partially grouted walls where only reinforced cells are filled).
   - The design designation: "fully grouted" (all cells filled) vs. "partially grouted" (only designated cells filled). This is a **design specification** defined on the structural plans.
2. Mark up a copy of the plan or create a cell map showing which cells at each pier, column, or wall segment require grout.
3. Confirm with the contractor that the grouting plan matches the structural plans.

### Step 2: Understand the Anatomy of a CMU Wall

Before inspecting, be clear on the distinction between two different types of voids in a CMU wall:

**Grout cells (structural):**
- The hollow cores within each CMU block unit.
- When designated on the plans, these cells receive vertical reinforcing steel and are filled with grout from above.
- Grout fills the cell cavity and encases the rebar, creating a reinforced concrete column within the masonry wall.

**Mortar joints (between units):**
- The horizontal and vertical joints between adjacent CMU blocks.
- These are filled with mortar during block laying (erection) — NOT with grout.
- Mortar joints transfer compression and shear between masonry units.
- The mortar joint is a different material, placed at a different time, by a different operation than cell grout.

**Why this distinction matters:** Deficiencies observed at the mortar joint interface between blocks (gaps, voids, missing mortar, filler materials) are a **mortar/block-laying workmanship issue**. Deficiencies inside the grout cell (incomplete fill, voids, lack of consolidation) are a **grout placement issue**. These have different causes, different code references, and different remediation paths. Conflating the two leads to incorrect diagnosis and misdirected corrective work.

### Step 3: Pre-Grout Cell Inspection

**This step must be completed before any grout is placed.**

1. Inspect each designated cell from above using a flashlight and mirror:
   - Cell must be **clean** — free of mortar droppings, debris, dirt, standing water, and foreign materials.
   - No mortar bridges or protrusions that could block grout flow within the cell.
   - No non-cementitious filler materials (foam, insulation, packing material, etc.) inside the cell cavity.
2. Verify vertical reinforcement is present in each designated cell:
   - Correct bar size per plans.
   - Bar is approximately centered in the cell — not pressed against the cell wall.
   - Bar extends the full required height with proper lap splices to bars below (if applicable).
3. For walls taller than **5 ft (60 in.)**, verify that **cleanout openings** are provided at the base of the wall per TMS 602-22 Section 3.5:
   - Cleanouts allow inspection and removal of debris from the bottom of tall cells before grouting.
   - Cleanout openings must be sealed before grout placement.
4. Document the pre-grout condition of cells with photographs.

### Step 4: Verify Grout Material

1. When the grout arrives (ready-mix delivery) or is mixed on-site, verify:
   - **Grout type:** Fine grout or coarse grout per the specifications.
     - Fine grout: Maximum aggregate size 3/8 in. — used for cells with minimum dimension less than 2 in. between rebar and cell wall.
     - Coarse grout: Maximum aggregate size 1/2 in. — used for larger cells (more common in standard 8-in. CMU).
   - **Slump:** Grout for masonry must have a slump of **8 to 11 inches** per ASTM C476 / TMS 602. This is much wetter than structural concrete (typically 4–6 in. slump). The high slump is required so grout flows around reinforcement and fills the cell completely.
     - If slump is low (thick, stiff mix), grout will not flow properly and voids are likely.
2. Collect delivery tickets and record:
   - Batch ticket number, date, time of delivery.
   - Mix design / specified strength (typically 2000 PSI minimum per TMS 602, or as specified on plans).
   - Volume delivered vs. volume required (calculated from number of cells × cell volume).
3. If the QA plan requires grout sampling, collect samples per ASTM C1019.

### Step 5: Observe Grout Placement

**The inspector should be present during grout placement.** This is a one-time operation — once grout is placed and begins to set, the internal condition of each cell is permanently concealed.

1. Verify **grout lift height** per TMS 602-22 Section 3.5 D:

   | Grout Type | Max. Pour Height (Lift) | Minimum Cell Dimension | Consolidation Method |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Fine grout | 12 ft 8 in. (if cell ≥ 1.5 in. × 2 in.) | 1.5 in. × 2 in. | Mechanical vibration or reconsolidation by rodding |
   | Coarse grout | 12 ft 8 in. (if cell ≥ 1.5 in. × 3 in.) | 1.5 in. × 3 in. | Mechanical vibration or reconsolidation by rodding |
   | Either type — low lift | 1 ft 4 in. max per pour | Per table | Puddling or rodding acceptable |

   *Note: Maximum lift heights depend on the grout space dimensions. Refer to TMS 602 Table 3.5 D for the specific requirements based on cell geometry.*

2. Observe that grout is placed **into each designated cell**:
   - Verify by watching the operation or by confirming grout is visible at the top of each designated cell after placement.
   - For tall pours, grout is placed in lifts with consolidation between lifts.

3. Verify **consolidation**:
   - Grout must be consolidated after placement by **mechanical vibration** (immersion vibrator) or by **rodding/puddling** for low-lift grouting.
   - For high-lift grouting (over 5 ft), mechanical vibration is standard practice.
   - Reconsolidation: After initial consolidation and before the grout takes initial set, the grout should be reconsolidated by vibrating to close settlement cracks and ensure full contact with the cell walls and rebar.
   - **Unconsolidated grout is one of the most common causes of internal voids in grouted masonry.** Grout placed without vibration can leave air pockets, bridging, and voids around reinforcement — even if the grout reaches the correct height at the top of the cell.

4. Monitor **timing between lifts**:
   - If grouting in multiple lifts, the time between lifts must comply with TMS 602 requirements to ensure bond between layers.
   - Do not allow grout to take initial set before the next lift is placed, unless the lift procedure accounts for construction joints.

### Step 6: Post-Placement Verification

1. After grout placement, verify that grout is visible at the top of all designated cells:
   - Grout should be at or near the top of the block course being grouted.
   - Any cell that shows no grout or a significantly low grout level should be flagged immediately — before the grout sets.
2. Check for grout leakage at the base of the wall or at mortar joints:
   - Some minor seepage at mortar joints is normal and indicates grout pressure reached the bottom of the cell.
   - Excessive leakage may indicate missing mortar in the bed/head joints, which could allow grout to escape the cell rather than filling it.
3. Document the completed grout placement with photographs showing grout visible at the top of cells.

### Step 7: Documentation

1. Record for each grouting operation:
   - Date, time, weather conditions.
   - Wall/pier/column identification (reference to plan grid or designation).
   - Cells grouted (by designation or count).
   - Grout material (type, delivery ticket number, specified strength).
   - Slump measurement (if taken).
   - Lift heights used.
   - Consolidation method observed (vibration, rodding, puddling).
   - Any anomalies observed during placement (low cells, leakage, mixing issues).
2. Photograph:
   - Pre-grout cell condition (clean cells with rebar visible).
   - Grout delivery and batch ticket.
   - Grout placement in progress.
   - Post-placement cells showing grout at top.
   - Any deficiencies observed.

## 8) Interpretation Criteria

| Result | Condition |
|---|---|
| **PASS** | All designated cells grouted to correct height, consolidation observed, grout material meets specifications, reinforcement verified in place prior to grout, delivery documentation complete |
| **HOLD** | Minor debris in cells correctable before grout, slump slightly outside range but correctable with adjustment, one cell missed — can be grouted before set |
| **FAIL** | Designated cells not grouted, grout placed without consolidation, reinforcement missing from required cells, non-cementitious filler found in structural cells, grout material does not meet specification, significant mortar joint failures allowing grout escape |

## 9) What AI Gets Wrong

1. **Confuses grout cells with mortar joints.** A common error in any masonry discussion — not just from AI — is conflating the grout inside CMU cells (placed from above into the hollow cores after block is laid) with the mortar between CMU units (placed during block laying to bond units together). These are different materials, placed at different times, serving different structural functions. A gap in a mortar joint is a block-laying deficiency. A void inside a grout cell is a grout placement deficiency. The causes, code references, and corrective actions are different. The Key Terminology table at the end of this protocol defines both.

2. **Assumes internal grout conditions can be reliably verified after curing.** Once grout is placed and cured, the internal condition of a CMU cell is permanently concealed. No post-construction method — visual or otherwise — can provide the same level of confidence as direct observation during placement. This is why grout verification is fundamentally a construction-phase activity, and why this protocol exists at this stage of the construction sequence.

3. **Treats "fully grouted" as a construction verification term rather than a design specification.** "Fully grouted" is a masonry design term (per TMS 402) meaning all cells in the wall are designated to receive grout, as opposed to "partially grouted" where only specific cells are filled. Whether the grouting was actually performed correctly is a separate question — that's a construction quality and workmanship matter verified through inspection during the grouting operation, batch documentation, and, when required, special inspection per the building code.

4. **Underestimates the importance of grout consolidation.** AI-generated masonry checklists frequently list "grout cells" as a single line item without addressing consolidation. Grout that is poured but not vibrated or rodded can bridge across reinforcement and cell geometry, leaving internal voids even though the grout reached the top of the cell. Consolidation is not optional — it is a code requirement per TMS 602.

5. **Assumes grout slump is the same as concrete slump.** LLMs often suggest slump values of 4–6 inches for masonry grout. Masonry grout requires **8 to 11 inches of slump** — far wetter than structural concrete — because it must flow into narrow cells around reinforcement. Using a low-slump mix leads to incomplete cell fill.

## 11) Escalation Path

| Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| Reinforcement missing from designated cells | Stop grout → cannot be corrected after grout is placed |
| Non-cementitious filler material found in structural cells | Fail → remove filler, clean cell, verify cell is clear before grout |
| Grout not consolidated (no vibration or rodding observed) | Hold → consolidate before initial set; if already set, flag for engineering review |
| Grout does not reach top of designated cells | Hold → add grout before initial set; if already set, flag for engineering review |
| Slump significantly outside 8–11 in. range | Hold → reject load or adjust; do not place non-conforming grout |
| Excessive grout leakage from mortar joints | Hold → repair joints, assess whether grout remained in cells |
| Designated cells grouted without inspector present (if special inspection required) | Flag → document that placement was not witnessed; engineer to determine if additional verification is needed |
| Post-construction concern about internal grout adequacy | Outside scope of this construction-phase protocol. Grout verification is a construction-time activity. Post-construction assessment of concealed grout conditions is a fundamentally different scope requiring destructive testing (coring, cell opening) with associated structural and repair implications → separate engagement with explicit scope definition required |

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Fully grouted** | A masonry design specification (TMS 402) indicating that ALL cells in the masonry element are designated to receive grout. This is a design intent — verification of execution is a construction inspection matter. |
| **Partially grouted** | A masonry design specification indicating that only DESIGNATED cells (typically those with reinforcement) receive grout. Undesignated cells remain hollow. |
| **Grout** | A cementitious mixture (per ASTM C476) placed into CMU cells to encapsulate reinforcement and create composite action between the masonry and steel. Placed from above after block is laid. High slump (8–11 in.). |
| **Mortar** | A cementitious mixture used to bond masonry units together at the head and bed joints during block laying. Different material, different placement time, different structural function than grout. |
| **Consolidation** | The process of vibrating or rodding grout after placement to remove air voids and ensure grout flows around reinforcement and fully fills the cell. Required by TMS 602. |
| **Reconsolidation** | A second consolidation pass performed after initial settlement but before initial set, to close settlement cracks and ensure full contact. |
| **Lift** | A single vertical pour of grout. Maximum lift height is governed by TMS 602 and depends on grout type and cell dimensions. |
| **Cleanout** | An opening at the base of a masonry wall (required for walls over 5 ft when using high-lift grouting) that allows inspection and debris removal before grouting. |
