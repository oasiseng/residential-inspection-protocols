## Purpose

This appendix provides the **detailed, step-by-step pressure testing procedure** for underground plumbing systems inspected under Protocol 301. It covers both the DWV (drain, waste, vent) test and the water supply pressure test as separate procedures with distinct acceptance criteria. This document is intended to be used **at the trench** during the test — it is written as a field reference, not a desk reference.

The pressure test is the **single most critical checkpoint** of the under-slab rough inspection. A leak discovered after backfill and slab placement requires cutting the slab — a catastrophic rework cost. Every step in this appendix exists to prevent that outcome.

---

## Preconditions (Before Starting Any Test)

- [ ] Protocol 301 Steps 1–9 are complete (piping, fittings, slope, joints, bedding verified).
- [ ] All piping is installed, supported, and exposed (not yet backfilled).
- [ ] All solvent-weld joints are fully cured — **minimum 24 hours** after the last joint was cemented (per cement manufacturer; longer in cold or humid conditions).
- [ ] Licensed plumber is on-site and will perform the test. Inspector witnesses.
- [ ] Pressure test apparatus is on-site: pump, gauges, caps, fittings, hose.
- [ ] Approved plans (sealed) are available for reference.
- [ ] All open pipe ends are **capped or plugged** with test caps rated for the test pressure.
- [ ] Ambient temperature is above 40°F (cement cure requirement; also affects gauge accuracy).

## Part 1: DWV (Drain, Waste, Vent) Pressure Test

The DWV system is tested to verify the integrity of all underground drain, waste, and vent piping before backfill and slab placement. Two test methods are accepted under FPC 312: the **water test** (hydrostatic) and the **air test** (pneumatic). The water test is preferred and most common for under-slab rough-in.

### Method A: DWV Water Test (Hydrostatic) — Preferred

#### Equipment Required

| Item | Specification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Test caps/plugs | Mechanical or inflatable, rated for water column pressure | One for each open pipe end (cleanouts, stubs, building sewer exit) |
| Water source | Garden hose with backflow preventer, or test pump with reservoir | Sufficient volume to fill the entire DWV system |
| Vent riser or standpipe | Clear pipe or tube extending to the highest point of the system | Allows filling to the required head and visual monitoring |
| Measuring stick or tape | 1/16 in. graduations | To measure water level in the vent riser |
| Timer | Stopwatch or phone timer | Minimum 15-minute hold period |
| Camera with timestamp | Date/time stamp visible | Photograph water level at start and end of hold period |
| Field notebook | — | Record all test parameters |

#### Procedure

**Step 1: Cap all openings.**
- Install test caps or mechanical plugs on every open pipe end: all fixture stubs, cleanout openings, building sewer exit.
- Verify each cap is hand-tight plus wrench-snug — no leaks at the cap itself.
- **Do not cap the highest vent opening** — this is where water will be filled to and where the water level will be monitored.

![Test caps installed on all DWV stub-outs before water fill](images/301a_dwv_test_caps_installed.jpg)

**Step 2: Fill the system with water.**
- Connect the water source to the lowest convenient opening (or fill from the vent riser top).
- Fill the DWV system slowly to avoid air pockets. Water should rise through the entire system and up the vent riser.
- Fill until the water level in the vent riser reaches the **top of the highest vent stack** (or 10 ft head, whichever is less, per FPC 312.2).
- Allow the system to stabilize for **5 minutes** after filling (to let air pockets dissipate and water temperature equalize).

**Step 3: Mark the initial water level.**
- Using a measuring stick or tape, mark the water level in the vent riser precisely.
- Photograph the water level with the measuring stick visible and a timestamp.
- Record: date, time, water level (e.g., "42.5 in. from top of vent riser"), ambient temperature.

![Initial water level marked on vent riser with measuring tape — start of 15-minute hold](images/301a_dwv_water_level_start.jpg)

**Step 4: Hold for 15 minutes.**
- Start the timer.
- **Inspector must remain at the vent riser and observe continuously during the entire 15-minute hold.** Do not leave the observation point.
- During the hold period, visually scan visible piping and joints for any signs of leakage: drips, weeping, wet spots on the trench bottom, water pooling near a joint.
- **Do not add water during the hold period.** Any topping-off invalidates the test.

**Step 5: Read the final water level.**
- At the 15-minute mark, read the water level using the same measuring stick/tape.
- Photograph the final water level with timestamp.
- Record: time, final water level.

**Step 6: Determine result.**

| Result | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **PASS** | No measurable drop in water level after 15 minutes. No visible leaks at any joint, cap, or fitting. |
| **FAIL** | Any measurable drop in water level, OR any visible leak at any point in the system. |

![Final water level at 15-minute mark — no drop, PASS](images/301a_dwv_water_level_end_pass.jpg)

**Step 7: If FAIL — locate and repair.**
- Drain the system.
- Inspect all joints, caps, and fittings for the source of the leak.
- Common failure points: insufficiently cemented joints (cold joints), cracked fittings, improperly seated test caps, damaged pipe.
- Repair the deficiency (cut out and replace joint, recement with proper primer/cement cure time).
- **Wait 24 hours** after repair for cement to cure.
- **Repeat the entire test from Step 1.**

### Method B: DWV Air Test (Pneumatic) — Alternative

> **WARNING:** Air testing DWV systems is inherently more dangerous than water testing. Compressed air stores energy; a rupture under pressure can propel pipe fragments. **Never exceed 5 PSI** for DWV air testing. The water test (Method A) is preferred.

#### Equipment Required

| Item | Specification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Test caps/plugs | Mechanical or inflatable, rated for air pressure | One for each open pipe end |
| Air compressor or hand pump | Low-pressure, regulated output | Must have a regulator that prevents exceeding 5 PSI |
| Pressure gauge | 0–15 PSI, 1/10 PSI graduations, ±0.5% accuracy | Must be calibrated within 12 months |
| Pressure relief valve | Set to 5 PSI maximum | Safety device to prevent over-pressurization |
| Timer | Stopwatch or phone timer | Minimum 15-minute hold period |
| Soapy water solution | Spray bottle | For leak detection at joints |
| Camera with timestamp | Date/time stamp visible | Photograph gauge at start and end |

#### Procedure

**Step 1: Cap all openings.**
- Install test caps or mechanical plugs on every open pipe end.
- **All openings must be capped** — there is no vent riser left open (unlike the water test).

**Step 2: Pressurize to 5 PSI.**
- Connect the air source to one convenient test port.
- Slowly pressurize the system to **5 PSI**. Do not exceed 5 PSI under any circumstances.
- Verify the pressure relief valve is set to 5 PSI and functioning.
- Allow the system to stabilize for **2 minutes** after reaching test pressure (air temperature equalization).

**Step 3: Record initial pressure.**
- Read the gauge: record the exact pressure (e.g., "5.0 PSI").
- Photograph the gauge with timestamp.
- Record: date, time, initial pressure, ambient temperature.

![DWV air test gauge at 5.0 PSI — start of 15-minute hold](images/301a_dwv_air_test_gauge_start.jpg)

**Step 4: Hold for 15 minutes.**
- Start the timer.
- Inspector must remain at the gauge and observe continuously.
- **Do not add air during the hold period.**
- While monitoring the gauge, the plumber may apply soapy water to joints to check for bubble formation (leak indication).

**Step 5: Read the final pressure.**
- At 15 minutes, read the gauge.
- Photograph with timestamp.
- Record: time, final pressure.

**Step 6: Determine result.**

| Result | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **PASS** | No pressure drop after 15 minutes. No bubbles at any joint when soapy water is applied. |
| **FAIL** | Any measurable pressure drop, OR bubbles visible at any joint/fitting/cap. |

**Step 7: If FAIL — depressurize, locate, repair, retest.**
- **Depressurize the system completely** before any repair work.
- Locate the leak (soapy water method or by listening for hissing).
- Repair, cure, and retest per the same procedure.

## Part 2: Water Supply Pressure Test (Hydrostatic)

The water supply system is tested separately from the DWV system. This test verifies the integrity of all underground hot and cold water supply piping (copper or PEX) before backfill and slab placement.

#### Equipment Required

| Item | Specification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrostatic test pump | Manual or electric, capable of 100+ PSI | Plumber-supplied; must have a shutoff and bleed valve |
| Pressure gauge | 0–200 PSI, 1/10 PSI graduations, ±0.5% accuracy | Calibrated within 12 months; must remain visible throughout test |
| Test caps | Rated for test pressure (100+ PSI) | On all open stub-outs (hot, cold, hose bibs) |
| Water source | Clean potable water | To fill and pressurize the system |
| Timer | Stopwatch or phone timer | Minimum 15-minute hold |
| Camera with timestamp | Date/time stamp visible | Photograph gauge at start and end |
| Thermometer (optional) | — | To note ambient and water temperature (affects PEX expansion) |

#### Procedure

**Step 1: Cap all supply line openings.**
- Install test caps rated for the test pressure on every open water supply stub-out (hot, cold, hose bibs, laundry, icemaker — every open end).
- Verify caps are secure.

**Step 2: Fill and purge air from the system.**
- Connect the test pump to one convenient stub-out or test port.
- Fill the water supply system with water, opening the highest point or farthest stub-out to purge air.
- Once water flows steadily from the purge point with no air bubbles, cap the purge point.
- **Air in the system will compress and give false readings.** Purging is essential.

**Step 3: Pressurize to test pressure.**
- Slowly pressurize the system to the required test pressure:
  - **Standard: 80 PSI** (FPC 312.5 — 1.5× the working pressure, or 80 PSI, whichever is greater).
  - **Some AHJs require 100 PSI.** Verify with the local AHJ before testing.
- Allow the system to stabilize for **5 minutes** after reaching test pressure.
  - PEX piping may show an initial pressure drop due to expansion under pressure — this is normal during the stabilization period. The 15-minute hold starts AFTER stabilization.

![Hydrostatic test pump connected to water supply stub-out, gauge visible](images/301a_water_test_pump_setup.jpg)

**Step 4: Record initial pressure.**
- Read the gauge: record the exact pressure (e.g., "80.0 PSI").
- Photograph the gauge with the reading clearly visible and a timestamp.
- Record: date, time, initial pressure, ambient temperature, water temperature (if noted).

![Water supply pressure gauge at 80 PSI — start of 15-minute hold](images/301a_water_supply_gauge_start.jpg)

**Step 5: Hold for 15 minutes.**
- Start the timer.
- **Inspector must remain at the gauge and observe continuously for the full 15 minutes.**
- During the hold, visually scan visible piping, joints, and fittings for any signs of leakage: drips, spray, wet spots, hissing sounds.
- **Do not add pressure during the hold period.** Any pump strokes during the hold invalidate the test.

> **PEX expansion note:** PEX piping expands slightly under sustained pressure, especially in warm ambient temperatures. A small initial drop (1–2 PSI) during the first 2–3 minutes of the stabilization period is expected. However, once the 15-minute hold begins (after stabilization), **no further drop is acceptable.** If you observe continued gradual decline during the hold, this indicates a leak — not expansion.

**Step 6: Read the final pressure.**
- At the 15-minute mark, read the gauge.
- Photograph with timestamp — the reading must be clearly legible in the photograph.
- Record: time, final pressure.

**Step 7: Determine result.**

| Result | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **PASS** | No pressure drop after 15 minutes (gauge reads the same as the initial reading after stabilization). No visible leaks at any joint, fitting, or cap. |
| **FAIL** | Any measurable pressure drop during the 15-minute hold, OR any visible leak at any point. |

![Water supply gauge at end of 15-minute hold — stable at 80 PSI, PASS](images/301a_water_supply_gauge_end_pass.jpg)

**Step 8: If FAIL — depressurize, locate, repair, retest.**
- Depressurize the system by opening the bleed valve on the test pump.
- Locate the leak:
  - **Copper:** Check all solder joints, fittings, and valve connections. Common failure: cold solder joint (insufficient heat during soldering).
  - **PEX:** Check all crimp/clamp ring connections, push-fit fittings, and manifold connections. Common failure: crimp ring not fully compressed, or fitting not fully seated.
- Repair the deficiency.
- **Repeat the entire test from Step 2** (including re-purging air).

## Common Test Failures and Root Causes

| Failure Pattern | Likely Root Cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| DWV water test — slow steady drop over 15 minutes | Small leak at a solvent-weld joint (cold joint or insufficient primer) | Drain system; inspect all joints with flashlight; look for wet spots at joint; repair and retest |
| DWV water test — rapid drop in first 2 minutes | Test cap not properly seated or blown off | Inspect all caps immediately; reseat or replace; refill and retest |
| DWV air test — slow gauge decline | Pinhole leak at fitting, or gasket not fully seated | Apply soapy water to all joints systematically; look for bubbles; repair and retest |
| DWV air test — gauge drops to zero quickly | Cap failure or major joint failure | Depressurize immediately; inspect all caps and major junctions; repair and retest |
| Water supply — 1–2 PSI drop during stabilization (first 5 min) | PEX expansion under initial pressurization (normal for PEX) | Allow full 5-minute stabilization; restart the 15-minute hold from the post-stabilization reading |
| Water supply — steady drop during 15-minute hold | Leak at a fitting or connection | Depressurize; inspect all crimp rings (PEX) or solder joints (copper); repair and retest |
| Water supply — pressure holds but visible drip at one joint | Pinhole leak at the joint; gauge may not show drop if leak is very slow | FAIL — even a visible drip is a failure. Repair the joint and retest |
| Any test — gauge fluctuates but doesn't drop | Temperature change affecting gauge reading (sun on gauge, cold water in warm pipe) | Shield gauge from direct sunlight; allow additional stabilization time; this is not a failure if the reading returns to the initial value |

## What AI Gets Wrong About Pressure Testing

1. **Treats the pressure test as a checkbox, not a witnessed procedure.** AI checklists consistently list "perform pressure test" as a single line item without specifying that the inspector must **witness the entire test duration continuously**, that the gauge must remain visible and readable throughout, and that no pressure/water can be added during the hold. The witnessed, continuous observation is the test — not the final reading alone.

2. **Does not differentiate between DWV and water supply test parameters.** The DWV test and water supply test have completely different pressures, methods, and acceptance criteria. DWV water test uses a water column (gravity head); DWV air test is capped at 5 PSI. Water supply test is 80–100 PSI. AI frequently conflates these or applies one set of parameters to both systems.

3. **Ignores PEX stabilization behavior.** PEX piping expands under sustained pressure, causing an initial pressure drop that is not a leak. AI responses do not mention the stabilization period or the need to re-read the gauge after stabilization before starting the 15-minute hold. Inspectors who follow AI guidance may fail a PEX system for normal expansion behavior, or worse, may ignore a real leak by attributing the drop to expansion.

4. **Allows the plumber to "top off" pressure during the hold.** Some AI-generated procedures suggest adding pressure or water if the reading drops slightly. This is **never acceptable** during the hold period — it masks leaks and invalidates the test. The entire purpose of the hold is to detect leaks through pressure/level decline. Any addition during the hold is a failed test.

5. **Omits the 24-hour cure requirement after joint repair.** When a joint fails and is repaired, the cement must cure for a minimum of 24 hours before retesting. AI guidance often says "repair and retest" without specifying the cure time, leading to retests on fresh cement that hasn't developed full bond strength — which can pass in the field and fail later under service conditions.
